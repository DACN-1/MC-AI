\chapter{Experiments}
\label{chap:experiments}

This chapter reports the $2\times2$ backbone-by-language ablation defined in
Section~\ref{ablation}, evaluated entirely in live MineRL rollouts. I first fix
the evaluation setup and a random-policy sanity floor
(Section~\ref{sec:exp-setup}), then present the two findings the design is built
to deliver: whether the language prompt measurably reshapes the policy
(Section~\ref{sec:exp-cond}), and how much of the demonstrated task each cell
actually completes (Section~\ref{sec:exp-task}). I then contrast these rollout
outcomes with the held-out frame metrics (Section~\ref{sec:exp-offline}), which
tell a strikingly different story. A final probe
(Section~\ref{sec:exp-film}) asks whether the weaker backbone fails because its
features are uninformative or because the head cannot read them. Throughout, I
treat the online rollout statistics of Section~\ref{sec:rollout} as primary and
report per-step firing rates from the recorded \texttt{steps} logs rather than
the per-step dominant-action summary, which undercounts simultaneously held
keys. Each cell is trained at \textbf{three independent seeds} (head
initialisation and data-order shuffle); every rollout number below is a mean over
the three seeds with the across-seed standard deviation as its uncertainty, so a
reported effect is one that survives retraining, not a single lucky run.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp-setup}

The four cells are the knob-free trajectory-split checkpoints
$\{\text{LLaVA},\text{CLIP}\}\times\{\text{lang},\text{nolang}\}$ of
Section~\ref{ablation}: Exp~1 (LLaVA + prompt), Exp~2 (CLIP + prompt), Exp~3
(LLaVA, no prompt) and Exp~4 (CLIP, no prompt). Every cell is evaluated under
four conditions that, crucially, all run in \emph{one fixed world}: the terrain is
identical across A, B, C and D, and the \textbf{prompt string} is the only
deliberately varied factor between them. This is a property of how the conditions
are seeded, not merely of the opening frame.
Episode $i$ of every condition is generated with the same world seed
(\texttt{base}${}+i$), applied to the same world generator: the two environment
ids in Table~\ref{tab:conditions} (\texttt{ChopATree640Fast} and
\texttt{CollectDirt640Fast}) are built from one shared world-generation
configuration and differ only in which item their reward handler counts --- a
quantity the agent never observes, since it reads only the RGB frame --- while
the generator's nominal biome flag has no effect in this MineRL build. Identical
seed and identical generator therefore yield the identical world for the whole
episode. Figure~\ref{fig:sameworld} confirms it at the first frame, which precedes
any action: the terrain in a dirt-task frame is identical to the matching
chop-task frame, the two differing only in the agent's own held tool at the bottom
edge and a single distant mob (mean absolute difference $2.6/255$, the same as
between any two same-condition episodes). The evaluation is thus a clean,
prompt-driven manipulation on a single scene, not a comparison across
environments.

Comparing conditions A, B and C within a single cell isolates the effect of the
prompt, since the world, the seed and the decoder are identical and only the
conditioning string changes. I quantify each binary key's firing rate per
condition with a Wilson $95\%$ confidence interval, compare conditions with a
two-proportion $z$-test, and report Cohen's $h$ as an effect size, exactly as
described in Section~\ref{sec:rollout}; per-cell rates are pooled over the three
training seeds and the across-seed standard deviation is carried as the headline
uncertainty. Because each condition contributes roughly $3\times10^4$ pooled
steps, the per-step intervals are tight and even small rate differences reach
nominal significance; I therefore lean on the effect size, the across-seed
spread and the random floor below, not on the $p$-value alone, to separate a
meaningful shift from a merely detectable one.

\paragraph{Random-policy floor.} To confirm that the trained agents act rather
than twitch, I run a random policy in the same environments at the same seed:
each binary key is flipped independently with probability $0.1$ and each camera
axis is drawn uniformly from $[-5^\circ,5^\circ]$, with no model in the loop.
The random policy fires every key at $9.6$--$10.5\%$ of steps, moves the camera
by $2.5^\circ$ on average, and collects \emph{zero} logs and \emph{zero} dirt
across all $20$ episodes. This is the chance baseline against which the trained
cells are read: Figure~\ref{fig:profile} shows every trained cell concentrating
its mass on \texttt{attack} and \texttt{forward} far above the flat $\sim\!10\%$
random line, confirming the policies diverge from chance before any
prompt-conditioning question is asked.

\section{Language Conditioning}
\label{sec:exp-cond}

The central question of the prompt ablation is whether the language string
changes what the agent does. Table~\ref{tab:conditioning} reports the
\texttt{attack} firing rate --- the most discriminative key --- across the three
chop-prompt conditions, averaged over the three training seeds, and
Figure~\ref{fig:conditioning} plots the same channel with across-seed error bars.

The effect is large and concentrated in a single cell, and it reproduces across
seeds. \textbf{CLIP + lang} suppresses its \texttt{attack} key from $80.4\%$
under the empty prompt to $38.2\%$ under \enquote{chop a tree}, a
$42.3\pm10.4$~percentage-point shift with $h=-0.91\pm0.24$ --- a large effect by
any threshold, present in all three seeds ($h=-1.04,-1.12,-0.57$), so it is a
property of the cell rather than of one lucky retraining. The prompt is the only
deliberately varied factor across the two conditions, and the world and seed are
held fixed, so the shift cannot be attributed to the scene or to rollout noise.
\textbf{CLIP $-$ lang}, the same backbone with the text branch zeroed, is flat:
\texttt{attack} moves by $+3.7\pm2.7$~pp ($h=+0.08$), within the noise band ---
exactly the negative control the design demands, since when language is removed
from the representation the prompt string can no longer act. I report the single
most discriminative key here; the wider reshaping of the action distribution
(steering, \texttt{sprint}) moves in the same direction but varies too much
across seeds to quantify cleanly, so I do not lean on it.

Both LLaVA cells are flat. \textbf{LLaVA + lang} shifts \texttt{attack} by only
$-3.0\pm3.7$~pp ($h=-0.06$) and \textbf{LLaVA $-$ lang} by $+2.3\pm4.9$~pp
($h=+0.05$); both averages sit inside the same band as the no-prompt controls,
and the across-seed spread straddles zero, so neither carries behavioural
meaning. The flatness is not a single-seed accident: no LLaVA seed exceeds
$|h|=0.16$, against CLIP + lang's weakest seed at $|h|=0.57$. The asymmetry noted
in Section~\ref{ablation} sharpens rather than weakens this reading: in the LLaVA
no-prompt cell the prompt still passes through the decoder and only the pooled
text vector is zeroed, so the pooled image features remain prompt-conditioned ---
yet even with that residual pathway, and even with the explicit text vector
present in the prompt cell, LLaVA never separates from its own control. The
defensible conclusion is therefore a statement about the backbone, not about
language in general: \emph{under this frozen-feature regime the contrastive CLIP
text encoder yields a conditioning signal the head uses, whereas the frozen
LLaVA-1.5 backbone does not} --- and, as the probe of Section~\ref{sec:exp-probe}
shows, not because the prompt is absent from its features but because the pooled
representation does not expose it as an axis a small head can act on.

\section{Task Performance}
\label{sec:exp-task}

Conditioning shows that a prompt changes behaviour; it does not show that the
behaviour completes the task. For task progress I use the reward-independent
peak-inventory measure of Section~\ref{sec:rollout}, the maximum count of the
goal item held during an episode. Chopping is at the floor for every cell ---
no cell collects more than a single log in any episode, in line with the broken
chop reward handler discussed in Section~\ref{sec:rollout} --- so the chop
conditions do not separate the models and I report dirt collection (condition D)
as the discriminating task. Table~\ref{tab:task} and Figure~\ref{fig:task} give
the per-episode results.

Task performance separates by backbone, not by language. \textbf{CLIP + lang}
collects $2.53\pm1.25$ dirt per episode and \textbf{CLIP $-$ lang} $1.60\pm0.50$;
both LLaVA anchor cells are at the floor, \textbf{LLaVA + lang} at $0.07\pm0.09$
(a hard zero in two of three seeds) and \textbf{LLaVA $-$ lang} at $0.47\pm0.45$,
the latter barely above the random policy's zero. The CLIP--versus--LLaVA gap is
the one task comparison robust to the sample: across three seeds and thirty
episodes the LLaVA anchors essentially never dig, while CLIP does so reliably.
The within-CLIP gap between the prompt and no-prompt cells ($2.53$ versus $1.60$)
is in the expected direction but \emph{soft} --- the across-seed intervals
overlap, so I cannot claim from the item count alone that the prompt improves
dirt collection. This is the honest limit of episode-level metrics at the sample
size the LLaVA backbone makes affordable, and it is why the conditioning analysis
of Section~\ref{sec:exp-cond}, which rests on ${\sim}3\times10^4$ per-step
observations and reproduces across seeds, carries the load-bearing
language-steering claim.

One caveat on this metric matters, because the dirt count is not a pure measure
of goal pursuit. Crucially, the comparison is \emph{not} confounded by the
environment: conditions A and D run in the same fixed world, with the surface
grass-over-dirt and the underground dirt veins generated identically, so dirt is
exactly as available under the empty prompt as under \enquote{collect dirt} (an
examiner's worry that ``plains simply contain more dirt than forest'' does not
apply, because there is only one scene). The dirt$_{\text{A}}$-versus-dirt
$_{\text{D}}$ contrast is thus a clean within-scene prompt manipulation. What it
shows is weaker than a single seed suggested, however. \textbf{CLIP + lang} digs
more under the dirt prompt than under the empty prompt on average ($1.2\to2.5$),
the direction goal-following predicts, but the per-seed lift is highly variable
($+3.5$, $-0.6$, $+1.2$) and its mean ($+1.4\pm1.7$) straddles zero; the strong
single-seed lift originally observed does not survive retraining. \textbf{CLIP
$-$ lang} shows no lift at all ($2.1\to1.6$), consistent with its dirt coming
from indiscriminate ground-attacking rather than goal pursuit. The honest reading
is therefore that a substantial part of every cell's dirt is incidental, so the
absolute count is not a clean task-following measure; the prompt-driven component
is visible only for CLIP + lang and only weakly at the episode level. The
reproducible evidence that the prompt steers CLIP behaviour is the firing-rate
conditioning of Section~\ref{sec:exp-cond}, not this item count.

\section{Offline Frame Metrics and the Offline--Online Gap}
\label{sec:exp-offline}

The rollout results stand in sharp contrast to how the four cells look on the
held-out test frames of Section~\ref{sec:split}. Table~\ref{tab:offline} reports
the offline metrics over the $156{,}541$-frame held-out test split --- a
trajectory-level split, so no trajectory contributes to both training and test ---
which yields $1{,}252{,}328$ per-step action predictions once each frame's
$8$-step action chunk is scored. On frames the four
cells are near-indistinguishable: binary-key accuracy is $0.98$ for all four,
\texttt{attack}-$F_1$ is $0.96$, camera bin accuracy is $0.87$ (against a
majority-class, always-still baseline of roughly $78\%$) and camera error is
below half a degree. Even the most behaviourally relevant offline number, the
movement-$F_1$ that selects the checkpoint (Section~\ref{caching}), spans only
$0.368$ to $0.431$ across the four --- and ranks them in an order the rollouts
flatly contradict: \textbf{CLIP $-$ lang} has the \emph{highest} movement-$F_1$
($0.431$) yet collects less dirt than \textbf{CLIP + lang}, and \textbf{LLaVA
$-$ lang} ($0.368$) sits within $0.04$ of \textbf{CLIP + lang} ($0.407$) yet is
at the collection floor.

This offline--online dissociation is the empirical core of the thesis's third
contribution. Held-out frame accuracy --- the metric most imitation-learning work
reports --- would rank these four backbones as interchangeable and would miss the
entire conditioning and task-collection story that appears only once the policy
is closed in the loop. It is a concrete instance of the known weak correlation
between offline action metrics and downstream task success~\cite{kanervisto2020}:
a single mispredicted key every few steps, invisible at the frame level, compounds
over a thousand-step episode into the difference between digging a stack of dirt
and digging nothing. The rollout evaluation is therefore not a costlier
restatement of the frame metrics but a qualitatively different measurement --- and
it is the one that separates the backbones.

\section{Features or Head? A FiLM Probe}
\label{sec:exp-film}

Read on its own, Section~\ref{sec:exp-task} invites the conclusion that the
frozen LLaVA features are useless for control. That conclusion would be too
strong, and two departures from the $2\times2$ show why. The LLaVA anchor feeds
the pooled text vector to the head by plain concatenation
(Section~\ref{action_head}). I test two alternative read-outs of the
\emph{same} frozen features: a feature-wise linear modulation (FiLM) head, in
which the text representation \emph{scales and shifts} the visual features rather
than sitting beside them, and a \enquote{fixAB} variant that adds a learned
affine recombination of the two pooled halves before the concatenating head.
Both lift the LLaVA + lang cell off the floor: the FiLM head collects
$2.60\pm1.07$ dirt per episode over three seeds and fixAB $2.5$ in a single run,
against the concatenating anchor's $0.07$ --- and FiLM's mean is statistically
on par with the best CLIP cell ($2.53\pm1.25$), the two intervals almost
coincident. That \emph{two distinct} read-outs both recover dirt collection from
features the concatenating head left at zero is the decisive point: the frozen
LLaVA representation \emph{does} carry the information needed to dig, so the
anchor's task failure is a read-out failure in the head, not an absence of signal
in the features.

Where that signal enters matters, and the no-prompt controls localise it. A FiLM
head gates the visual features on the text vector, so zeroing that vector should
disable the gate --- and it does: the no-language FiLM cell falls back to the
floor ($0.20$ dirt). The fixAB head, which keeps a concatenation path, partially
survives without language ($1.50$). So FiLM's recovery is routed specifically
\emph{through} the text vector; the task signal the better heads exploit is the
task-identifying content of that vector, not a generic capacity gain.

Crucially, none of this recovers \emph{conditioning}. Every one of these heads
leaves \texttt{attack} flat across the prompt --- FiLM + lang shifts it by only
$-3.2\pm4.5$~pp, fixAB by $-0.2$, the no-language controls likewise --- exactly
the band of every other LLaVA cell. A better head unlocks the \emph{task} signal
in the LLaVA features but not a prompt-driven \emph{conditioning} signal. That a
head which multiplicatively gates the visual features on the text vector still
does not condition is itself informative: it indicates the limit is not head
capacity but the pooled text representation, which does not expose a prompt axis
to gate on (Section~\ref{sec:exp-probe}). These probes thus separate two
bottlenecks --- the LLaVA \emph{task} floor lives in the head and a better
read-out clears it, whereas the LLaVA \emph{conditioning} failure lives upstream
of the head, in the pooled representation. The FiLM and fixAB cells, the
no-language controls especially, are single-seed probes rather than full members
of the $2\times2$; only FiLM + lang is replicated across three seeds. I return to
the implications of this split in Chapter~\ref{chap:discussion}.

\section{Is the Prompt Represented? A Linear Probe}
\label{sec:exp-probe}

Is the LLaVA conditioning failure a missing signal or an unusable one? A cheap
offline probe narrows it down. On the cached training features I fit a logistic
regression to predict the task --- equivalently, which training prompt was given
--- from the image half of the pooled feature, the text half, and the full
vector, on a balanced $30{,}000$-frame sample (Table~\ref{tab:probe}).

The task is almost perfectly decodable from the pooled LLaVA text half ($99.7\%$),
above the $90.1\%$ reachable from the image half, and it collapses to chance
($50\%$) when the text half is zeroed: the prompt is far from washed out of the
representation. But decodability alone does not make the prompt \emph{usable} for
conditioning, because in the combined training set the two prompts are perfectly
correlated with the two tasks' imagery (tree-felling scenes versus dirt-digging
scenes) and the pooled LLaVA text half is image-entangled --- its tokens attend
to the frame inside the decoder. The probe cannot separate a genuine prompt axis
from the image content that leaks into the text positions. This training-time
entanglement is exactly what the rollout evaluation breaks apart: there all four
prompts are issued in one fixed scene, decorrelating prompt from image, and the
LLaVA policy does not move --- evidence that the separable prompt axis is absent,
not merely masked.

Which it is --- a usable axis or a confound --- can be measured directly. The
probe above leaves it ambiguous because each cached frame is seen under exactly
one prompt, so prompt and scene co-vary; the FiLM null of
Section~\ref{sec:exp-film} already \emph{indicates} a confound (a head that gates
on the text vector still does not condition), but a counterfactual settles it. I
take a fixed set of $256$ held-out frames (from the chop trajectories) and
re-encode every one under all four eval prompts, then ask how far each half of the
pooled feature moves when only the prompt changes at a fixed image, against how
far it moves across images at a fixed prompt (Table~\ref{tab:swap}). The
conclusion is a backbone property and does not depend on the frames' own task ---
each frame is scored under the off-scene prompts too.

The two backbones separate exactly as the conditioning result predicts.
\textbf{CLIP}'s text half is a clean prompt axis: swapping the prompt at a fixed
frame moves it substantially (cosine $0.74$, and $0.73$/$0.73$ for the on-task
chop and dirt prompts against the empty one), while swapping the frame at a fixed
prompt leaves it untouched (cosine $1.00$), because its text encoder never sees
the image. \textbf{LLaVA} inverts this: swapping the prompt barely moves the text
half ($1\%$ relative-$L_2$, cosine $1.00$ even between the chop and dirt prompts),
while swapping the frame does move it (cosine $0.98$) --- and its image half is
strongly frame-dependent (cosine $0.49$ across frames) yet equally
prompt-invariant. The prompt simply does not register in LLaVA's pooled features;
what the linear probe decoded at $99.7\%$ was the \emph{scene} (chop frames differ
from dirt frames, and each came with its own prompt), not a prompt axis. This is
the direct confirmation, by measurement rather than inference, that the LLaVA
conditioning bottleneck is the pooled, image-dominated representation upstream of
the head, not the head itself.

\section{Summary of Findings}
\label{sec:exp-summary}

\begin{itemize}
\item All four cells diverge sharply from a random policy, which fires every key
at $\sim\!10\%$ and collects nothing; the trained agents are genuine policies,
not noise (Figure~\ref{fig:profile}).
\item Language conditioning is a backbone effect that reproduces across seeds.
Only CLIP + lang reshapes its policy with the prompt (\texttt{attack}
$-42.3\pm10.4$~pp, $h=-0.91$, large in all three seeds); its no-prompt control
and both LLaVA cells stay within the $|h|\le0.16$ noise band
(Table~\ref{tab:conditioning}). The prompt is not missing from LLaVA's features
--- a linear probe recovers the task from its pooled text half at $99.7\%$
(Section~\ref{sec:exp-probe}) --- but it is entangled with the image, and no
head-side lever, the FiLM gate included, turns it into conditioning; the
bottleneck is the pooled representation, not the head.
\item Task performance also separates by backbone: CLIP collects dirt
($2.53\pm1.25$ and $1.60\pm0.50$ per episode) while both LLaVA anchor cells are at
the floor ($0.07$, $0.47$; Table~\ref{tab:task}). The within-CLIP prompt effect
is in the right direction but soft at the episode level; chopping fails for every
model. Because all conditions share one fixed world, the dirt count is not
confounded by environment, but a substantial part of it is incidental digging, so
the reproducible prompt-steering evidence is the firing-rate conditioning, not the
item count.
\item Held-out frame metrics do not predict rollout behaviour: all four cells
score within $0.04$ movement-$F_1$ and $0.001$ binary accuracy offline, yet
diverge from a dug stack of dirt to nothing online --- a direct demonstration
that rollout evaluation measures what frame metrics miss
(Section~\ref{sec:exp-offline}, Figure~\ref{fig:dissociation}).
\item The LLaVA floor is a head limitation, not a feature limitation: two
alternative read-outs (FiLM, fixAB) lift dirt collection from $0.07$ to ${\sim}2.5$
per episode --- FiLM to $2.60\pm1.07$, on par with the CLIP cells --- from the
same frozen features, without recovering conditioning. FiLM does so specifically
through the text vector (its no-language control returns to the floor), localising
the task signal while the conditioning bottleneck stays upstream
(Section~\ref{sec:exp-film}).
\end{itemize}
